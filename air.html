Batteries


Aviation fuels are usually petroleum-based such as kerosene or jet fuel. Similarly to petrol or diesel, when these fuels are combusted in the engine of the plane, CO2 is produced along with the condensed water we see as vapour trails.

Batteries could be a good option for powering smaller aircraft such as private planes or short-haul commercial aircraft. However, to date, the largest commercial aircraft to be powered by batteries was only able to carry nine passengers and flew for only 30 minutes. As planes are usually grounded for a long time between flights, long recharge times may be considered acceptable for commercial planes. However, batteries are far too heavy to be placed on the larger 200-300 seat commercial planes that we are used to flying in today (they would struggle to even get off the ground!), so another type of fuel is required to make a significant reduction to CO2 emissions in the aviation industry.

There is also a safety concern around lithium batteries as they can catch on fire if overheated. This is the same reason why airlines are very careful about allowing you take on board batteries for mobile devices.


  • No gaseous emissions
  • Can be charged directly from renewable sources
  • Long recharge times (hours)
  • Batteries are heavy
  • Batteries are made of expensive precious metals
  • Batteries cannot be recycled

Sustainable Aviation Fuel


Sustainable aviation fuels (SAF) are biofuels that have very similar chemical composition to existing aircraft fuels, but are made from a renewable sources rather than fossil fuels. This can be waste cooking oils and animal fats that would otherwise be incinerated, or from dedicated energy crops (although space to grow these crops and required destruction of natural habitats makes this option less favourable).

The use of SAF would greatly reduce the carbon footprint from the aviation industry and in most cases, it can be blended with traditional aviation fuels or replace it completely, without needing to replace engines and refuelling infrastructure. However, SAF is more expensive than conventional aviation fuel, hence why it is not widely uptaken by the aviation industry.


  • Works with existing engines
  • Can use existing refuelling infrastructure
  • Carbon dioxide is still produced at the exhaust
  • Some SAF need to be blended with conventional fuels
  • Limited supply due to available feedstock

Hydrogen



  • Only water vapour produced at the exhaust
  • Fast refuelling time
  • Long driving range
  • Hydrogen production method may still produce carbon dioxide
  • Requires expensive fuel cell system
  • Lack of fuelling infrastructure
